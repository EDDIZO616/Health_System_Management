Introduction to Health Systems Management

▼

1.1 What is Health Systems Management?

Health Systems Management (HSM) describes the leadership and general management of hospitals, hospital networks, and healthcare systems. It involves the planning, organization, and monitoring of healthcare resources and services to efficiently meet population needs.

A health service manager is responsible for the provision of local healthcare through the management of hospital, general practitioner (GP), and community health services. Managers liaise with clinical and non-clinical staff and other partner organizations while considering the demands of political policy and local circumstances.

1.2 Key Definitions

TermDefinition
SystemA collection of components that work together to achieve a common objective
Health SystemThe sum of the organizations, institutions, and resources whose shared primary purpose is to improve health (WHO)
Health Information SystemA system that integrates data collection, processing, reporting, and use of information necessary for improving health service effectiveness and efficiency
Health Management Information SystemAn information system specially designed to assist in the management and planning of health programmes

1.3 The WHO Health System Building Blocks

The World Health Organization (WHO) framework identifies six fundamental building blocks that constitute a health system:

🏨
Service Delivery

Effective, safe, quality health services

👩‍⚕️
Health Workforce

Responsive, fair, and efficient health workers

📊
Health Information Systems

Reliable data for decision-making

💊
Medical Products, Vaccines & Technologies

Access to essential medicines

💳
Health Financing

Adequate funds; protection from financial hardship

🏛️
Leadership & Governance (Stewardship)

Strategic policy frameworks, regulation, accountability

🔑 Exam Memory Hook
Remember the WHO 6 Building Blocks as: S-H-I-M-F-L — Service Delivery, Health Workforce, Information Systems, Medical Products, Financing, Leadership/Governance. Or use: "Some Health Issues Mean Financial Loss."

1.4 The Five Core Pillars of HSM

Health Systems Management rests on five interconnected pillars:

PillarDescription
Leadership/GovernanceStrategic direction, policy development, and accountability structures
Health FinancingRaising and allocating funds for health services
Service DeliveryOrganizing and providing effective, safe, quality care
Workforce ManagementRecruiting, training, and retaining health workers
Health Information SystemsData collection, analysis, and use for decision-making

The Need for Managers in Healthcare

▼

2.1 Why Healthcare Organizations Need Managers

Healthcare is an expansive and complex industry that ranges from preventative care to emergency services, follow-up, and rehabilitation. Healthcare organizations are complex and dynamic, requiring managers to provide leadership, supervision, and coordination.

2.2 Key Reasons for Management in Healthcare

🎯
Beyond Individual Capacity

Organizations achieve goals beyond any single individual's reach. The scope of healthcare tasks demands collective management.

⚡
Resource Optimization

Managers ensure that organizational tasks are carried out in the best way possible to achieve goals.

🧩
Resource Adequacy

Managers ensure that appropriate financial and human resources are adequate to support the organization.

🔗
Coordination & Integration

Managers coordinate diverse departments and services to work towards common goals.

🏅
Quality Assurance

Managers establish systems to monitor and improve the quality of care.

2.3 Managerial Roles in Health Services

There is a huge range of managerial roles within health services:

💰 Finance Management
👥 Human Resources Management
🩺 Clinical Management
👷 Staff Management
📋 Project Management
🛒 Procurement Management
📊 Information Management
🏗️ Facilities Management
⚙️ Operational Management

2.4 What Health Systems Management Ensures

Health systems management ensures that:

  • Specific outcomes are attained
  • Departments within a health facility are running smoothly
  • Right people are in the right jobs
  • People know what is expected of them
  • Resources are used efficiently
  • All departments are working towards a common goal for mutual development and growth

Management Theories & Principles

▼

3.1 Overview

Management theories provide frameworks for understanding how organizations function and how to effectively lead people. Over the past 80 years, organization theorists have formulated three main conceptual frameworks to examine the relationship between employees and their work environment.

3.2 Classical Management Theories

3.2.1 Scientific Management (Taylorism) – 1900–1930

Founder: Frederick Winslow Taylor

Core Principles
Taylorism
  • Focus on maximizing task efficiency and production
  • Work environment as a set of conditions for ensuring task performance and controlling employees
  • Little regard for interpersonal issues or individual differences
  • Emphasis on standardization and division of labor
  • "Shop-floor" focus on worker behaviour
Healthcare Application
In Practice
  • Standardized clinical protocols and procedures
  • Time-and-motion studies in hospital operations
  • Task specialization (nurses, technicians, doctors)
Criticisms
  • Treats workers as interchangeable parts of a machine
  • Neglects human needs and motivation
  • Can lead to employee alienation

3.2.2 Administrative Management

Founder: Henri Fayol

Fayol's 14 Principles of Management:

#Principle
1Division of work
2Authority and responsibility
3Discipline
4Unity of command
5Unity of direction
6Subordination of individual interests
7Remuneration
8Centralization
9Scalar chain (hierarchy)
10Order
11Equity
12Stability of tenure
13Initiative
14Esprit de corps (team spirit)

Healthcare applications: Hierarchical hospital structures, clear lines of authority and reporting, departmental organization.

3.2.3 Bureaucratic Principles

Founder: Max Weber

  • Clear hierarchy of authority
  • Written rules and procedures
  • Division of labour based on specialization
  • Impersonal relationships
  • Career advancement based on merit

Healthcare applications: Hospital organizational charts, standard operating procedures, credentialing and licensing systems.

3.3 Human Relations Approach – 1930–1970

Key Figures: Elton Mayo, Mary Parker Follett, Abraham Maslow, Douglas McGregor

3.3.1 Core Principles

The human relations approach was shaped by concern about employee alienation and the conviction that a narrow focus on productivity could lead to poorer job performance. It emphasizes:

  • The value of individual and small group relationships
  • Organizational development and quality of work life
  • Social needs, motivation, and behaviour of people within organizations

3.3.2 Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Maslow's Hierarchy (bottom → top)
  1. Physiological needs — Food, water, shelter
  2. Safety needs — Security, stability
  3. Love/Belonging needs — Social connection, team
  4. Esteem needs — Recognition, status
  5. Self-actualization — Reaching full potential

3.3.3 McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y

Theory X (Traditional)Theory Y (Human Relations)
People dislike workPeople view work as natural
Must be coerced/controlledWill exercise self-direction
Avoid responsibilitySeek responsibility
Need close supervisionCan be creative and innovative

3.3.4 Healthcare Applications of Human Relations

  • Patient-centred care approaches
  • Team-based care models
  • Staff engagement and motivation programmes
  • Recognition and reward systems
  • Participative decision-making

3.4 Socio-Technical Approach – 1970s onwards

Key Principle: Encompasses both the technological/task attributes of a job and the interpersonal and organizational context in which it is performed.

Healthcare applications:

  • Electronic Health Record (EHR) implementation considering staff workflows
  • Balancing technology with human interaction in patient care
  • System redesign that considers both technical and social factors

3.5 Contemporary Management Theories

4.1 What is Health Policy?

Health policy refers to decisions, plans, and actions undertaken to achieve specific healthcare goals within a society. It defines a vision for the future and outlines priorities, expected roles of different groups, and the regulatory framework.

Strategic planning is the process of defining organizational direction and making decisions on allocating resources to pursue this direction.

4.4 Managing Organizational Change

Change is inevitable in healthcare due to: advances in medical technology, changing population health needs, policy reforms, and resource constraints.

Kotter's 8-Step Change Model

1
Create urgency

Help others see the need for change and the importance of acting immediately

2
Form a powerful coalition

Assemble a group with enough power to lead the change effort

3
Create a vision for change

Clarify how the future will be different from the past

4
Communicate the vision

Use every vehicle possible to communicate the new vision and strategies

5
Remove obstacles

Empower people to act on the vision and remove barriers

6
Create short-term wins

Plan for and create visible performance improvements

7
Build on the change

Use credibility to change systems, structures, and policies that don't fit the vision

8
Anchor the changes in corporate culture

Articulate connections between new behaviors and organizational success

4.5 Devolution of Health Services

Devolution is the transfer of power and decision-making authority from central to sub-national levels of government.

Key Aspects of Devolution in Health

  • Bringing decision-making closer to service delivery
  • Expanding participation in sub-national health management
  • Improving equity and bringing specialized services closer to communities

Challenges of Devolution

  • Complicated and politicized decision-making
  • Planning and budgeting challenges at county/sub-national levels
  • Need for capacity building at local levels
  • Coordination between levels of government
🇰🇪 Kenya Example
Kenya adopted a devolved system of government where health services delivery became a function of 47 semi-autonomous county governments. Devolution represented a concerted attempt to bring decision-making closer to service delivery.

Human Resource Management in Healthcare

▼

5.1 What is Human Resource Management (HRM)?

HRM in healthcare involves the recruitment, training, development, and retention of healthcare professionals. It encompasses all aspects of managing the workforce to ensure high-quality patient care.

5.2 Key HRM Functions in Healthcare

FunctionDescription
Workforce PlanningForecasting staffing needs
RecruitmentAttracting qualified candidates
SelectionChoosing the right people for the right jobs
Training & DevelopmentBuilding skills and competencies
Performance ManagementEvaluating and improving performance
Compensation & BenefitsFair remuneration and rewards
Employee RelationsManaging workplace relationships
RetentionKeeping valued employees

5.3 Recruitment Challenges in Healthcare

HR managers face multiple recruitment challenges:

Recruitment Challenges
  • Talent shortages — Limited supply of qualified healthcare professionals
  • Lengthy recruitment processes — Slow hiring procedures
  • Biased hiring practices — Unfair selection processes
  • Weak employer branding — Inability to attract top talent
Retention Challenges
  • Poor leadership — Ineffective management
  • Lack of employee motivation — Low morale and engagement
  • Inadequate compensation — Uncompetitive pay and benefits
  • Burnout — High stress and workload
  • Limited career development — Few advancement opportunities

5.4 Strategies for Improving Retention

🏆
Reward & Recognition

Recognise and reward excellent performance

💵
Competitive Compensation

Competitive pay, benefits and allowances

📚
Training & Development

Continuous learning and skills building

⚖️
Work-Life Balance

Job flexibility and manageable workloads

🚀
Career Pathways

Clear advancement and promotion opportunities

🤝
Mentorship Programmes

Pairing new staff with experienced mentors

🌟
Strong Workplace Culture

Positive, respectful and inclusive environment

📢
Clear Communication

Transparent communication of career advancement

5.5 Factors Influencing Healthcare Worker Performance

Research has revealed that the following significantly influence staff performance:

  • Employee remuneration — Fair pay
  • Management styles — Leadership approach
  • Recruitment and selection practices — Quality of hiring
  • Workplace design — Physical and psychological environment

5.6 Creating a Positive Working Climate

Supportive work environments and strong managerial backing significantly enhance motivation and performance, ultimately improving patient care quality.

Financial Management & Universal Health Coverage

▼

6.1 What is Healthcare Financial Management?

Healthcare financial management is concerned with accounting and budgeting, along with the related reporting and analysis that make it possible to ensure that the organization's resources are used in the service of its mission, for maximum impact, in compliance with laws and donor requirements, and in accordance with ethical standards.

6.2 Key Components

📋
Budgeting

Planning for future financial activities

🔍
Resource Mobilization

Raising funds for health services

📊
Financial Reporting

Tracking and communicating financial performance

🔒
Internal Controls

Safeguarding assets and ensuring accountability

🔎
Auditing

Independent verification of financial records

6.3 Types of Healthcare Budgets

Budget TypeDescription
Operating BudgetDay-to-day expenses (salaries, supplies, utilities)
Capital BudgetLong-term investments (equipment, facilities)
Cash BudgetCash flow projections
Programme BudgetSpecific programmes or services

6.4 Sources of Healthcare Financing

  • Government funding — Tax revenue allocation
  • Health insurance — Social and private insurance schemes
  • Out-of-pocket payments — Direct patient payments
  • Donor funding — International aid and grants
  • Public-private partnerships — Collaborative financing arrangements

6.5 Universal Health Coverage (UHC)

📖 Definition
Universal Health Coverage means that all people have access to the full range of quality health services they need, when and where they need them, without financial hardship.

Key Dimensions of UHC

Three Dimensions
  • Population coverage — Who is covered?
  • Service coverage — What services are covered?
  • Financial protection — How much do people pay?
Requirements for Achieving UHC
  • Increased funding for the health sector
  • Efficient, equitable, and accountable use of resources
  • Effective public financial management
  • Sustainable financial systems

Public Financial Management and UHC

"Achieving UHC requires not only increased funding for the health sector but also the efficient, equitable, and accountable use of resources."
PrincipleMeaning
AccountabilityTransparency in resource use
EfficiencyGetting maximum value from resources
EquityFair distribution of resources
SustainabilityLong-term financial viability

6.6 Financial Management in Practice

"The budget is not just numbers on paper—it is a reflection of our collective commitment to better health outcomes."

Effective financial management requires:

  • Strategic alignment — Budgets aligned with health priorities
  • Stakeholder engagement — Involving all relevant parties
  • Monitoring and evaluation — Tracking financial performance
  • Continuous improvement — Learning and adapting

Total Quality Management (TQM)

▼

7.1 What is Total Quality Management?

Total Quality Management (TQM) is a strategic management approach that aims to improve healthcare services by enhancing operational efficiency and ensuring patient satisfaction. TQM in healthcare is a management approach focused on continuously improving the quality of patient care and operational efficiency.

"TQM has become a globally recognized strategic management philosophy, focusing on customer satisfaction and fostering a cohesive strategy to enhance organizational performance."

7.2 Key Principles of TQM

PrincipleDescription
Customer FocusPrioritizing patients as ultimate customers; meeting their needs and enhancing satisfaction
Continuous Quality Improvement (CQI)Ongoing enhancement of processes, services, and outcomes
Employee InvolvementParticipation of all members of the organization
Process OrientationSystematic process improvements
TeamworkCollaboration among healthcare providers; interdisciplinary teams working together
Data-Driven Decision-MakingUsing evidence and data to guide improvements
Long-Term CommitmentSustained effort over time
Education and TrainingBuilding capacity for quality

2

📊 Research Evidence
Research shows that continuous quality improvement positively and significantly predicts patient satisfaction in healthcare settings.

7.5 TQM and Patient Satisfaction

Patient satisfaction is a core determinant of healthcare success and directly reflects the effectiveness of quality improvement measures.

  • TQM fosters a culture of quality, aligning staff efforts with organizational goals
  • Quality improvement initiatives should be aligned with customer expectations
  • TQM aims to enhance patient satisfaction and outcomes through systematic process improvements

Summary & Exam Preparation Tips

▼

8.1 Key Takeaways

  • Health Systems Management is the leadership and management of healthcare organizations to efficiently meet population health needs.
  • The WHO Building Blocks provide a comprehensive framework for understanding health systems: Service Delivery, Health Workforce, Information, Medical Products, Financing, and Leadership/Governance.
  • Managers are essential in healthcare because organizations are complex and tasks exceed individual capacity.
  • Management theories evolved from Scientific Management (efficiency-focused) through Human Relations (people-focused) to contemporary approaches that consider both.
  • Health Policy and Strategic Planning involve formulating objectives, managing change, and navigating devolution.
  • Human Resource Management addresses recruitment, motivation, retention, and creating a positive working climate.
  • Financial Management ensures resources are used efficiently and effectively toward Universal Health Coverage.
  • Total Quality Management focuses on continuous improvement, patient satisfaction, and a culture of quality.

8.2 Exam Preparation Tips

📖 Theory Questions
  • Memorize the WHO six building blocks and the five pillars of HSM
  • Understand the evolution of management theories and their healthcare applications
  • Know the key principles of each management approach
🔬 Application Questions
  • Apply theories to real healthcare scenarios
  • Understand devolution and its implications for health services
  • Know strategies for recruitment and retention of health workers
  • Understand the link between financial management and UHC
✍️ Essay Questions
  • Use the PEEL structure (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link)
  • Integrate examples from the notes
  • Link concepts across different topics (e.g., how HRM supports TQM)
  • Reference the WHO framework as a unifying concept
✍️ PEEL Essay Structure
Point — State your argument clearly
Evidence — Provide facts, theories, or examples
Explanation — Explain how the evidence supports your point
Link — Connect back to the question or to the next point
